> existing codebase, someone else's decisions — where most of us actually spend our careers.

## real talk

A brownfield project means working within an existing codebase, with existing architectural decisions, existing technical debt, and existing conventions you didn't choose — and often can't fully explain, because whoever made those decisions left the company two years ago and the context left with them. This is genuinely most of professional software development, despite greenfield projects getting all the exciting job posting language.

Brownfield work requires a different skill set than greenfield: reading and understanding unfamiliar code, making safe incremental changes without fully rewriting things, and accepting that "why is it built this way" sometimes has a real historical reason and sometimes has no reason at all beyond deadline pressure from years ago.

## used in the wild

> "the job posting said 'exciting greenfield opportunity' and it was actually a 6-year-old brownfield codebase with zero tests. read the fine print next time."
>
> — engineer, learning a valuable lesson about job posting language

## why it matters

Most real engineering value gets created in brownfield work — carefully improving something that already matters to real users, rather than starting fresh. It's less glamorous and arguably more valuable than greenfield work gets credit for.

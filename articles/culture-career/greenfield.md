> a brand-new project, no legacy to deal with — exciting for one month, then you make the mess yourself.

## real talk

A greenfield project starts from a literal blank slate: no existing codebase, no legacy decisions to work around, no "why is this built this way" archaeology required. You pick the stack, the architecture, the conventions — total freedom. This is genuinely exciting and rare in most careers, because most software jobs involve maintaining something that already exists.

The slightly humbling part everyone discovers eventually: greenfield projects don't stay clean forever. Given six months of real deadlines, scope changes, and rushed decisions, every greenfield project eventually becomes someone else's brownfield project. The "legacy mess" you're often hired to fix was, at some point, somebody's exciting greenfield opportunity too.

## used in the wild

> "we're starting fresh, totally greenfield, no legacy constraints. let's make good decisions this time."
>
> — every team, at the start of every greenfield project, with complete sincerity

## why it matters

Recognizing that greenfield freedom is temporary keeps you honest about the decisions you're making — today's clean, fast shortcut is tomorrow's legacy mess for whoever inherits this project next.

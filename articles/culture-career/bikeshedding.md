> wasting hours debating trivial things (tab size, button color) while the actually hard decision goes undiscussed.

## real talk

Named from a parable about a committee designing a nuclear power plant — they spent five minutes approving the multi-million dollar reactor design, then forty-five minutes arguing about the color of the employee bike shed, because everyone has an opinion about bike sheds and almost nobody feels qualified to challenge nuclear engineering. Same energy shows up constantly in code reviews and meetings: the hard, important architectural decision sails through with a shrug, while everyone has a strong, time-consuming opinion about variable naming or whether to use single or double quotes.

The trivial thing is easy to have an opinion about and feels safe to argue over. The hard thing is intimidating and risks looking uninformed, so people quietly avoid engaging with it.

## used in the wild

> "we spent 40 minutes on whether to call it `userId` or `user_id` and then approved the entire database migration strategy in two minutes. classic bikeshedding."
>
> — engineer, reflecting on a meeting that went exactly backwards

## why it matters

Recognizing bikeshedding in real time lets you redirect a meeting back toward the decision that actually matters, instead of letting the easy, low-stakes argument eat all the available time and energy.

> highest paid person's opinion wins, regardless of what the data actually says — extremely common in meetings.

## real talk

HiPPO stands for "Highest Paid Person's Opinion," and the HiPPO effect describes decisions getting made based on hierarchy and confidence rather than evidence. The data shows users prefer option A, the A/B test results are clear, the user research backs it up — and then a senior exec says "I just have a feeling B is better" and B ships, because seniority outranked the actual evidence in the room.

This isn't always wrong — sometimes senior people have genuine pattern-matching from experience that data can't fully capture. But it's a well-documented failure mode in product and engineering decisions, and naming it explicitly ("isn't this just a HiPPO decision?") is sometimes the only socially acceptable way to push back on a bad call from someone senior.

## used in the wild

> "the A/B test ran for three weeks and version A won by a wide margin. we're shipping version B anyway because the VP prefers it. classic HiPPO effect."
>
> — PM, watching data lose to hierarchy in real time

## why it matters

Naming the HiPPO effect explicitly gives teams a shared vocabulary to question decisions that are being made on authority rather than evidence, without it sounding like a personal attack on whoever's making the call.
